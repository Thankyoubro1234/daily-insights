# How to Actually Compare TVs Before You Buy: A 2026 Spec-by-Spec Guide

Buying a TV in 2026 is harder than it should be. Every manufacturer publishes 30+ specifications, half of them are marketing inventions, and the spec sheet rarely tells you how the TV will actually look in *your* living room. After spending months building structured product comparisons over at [VersusNest](https://versusnest.com), I've boiled the decision down to seven specs that actually matter — and a handful of "specs" you can safely ignore.

This guide is meant to be the comparison framework I wish I'd had the first time I dropped $1,500 on a TV I ended up returning.

## TL;DR — the seven specs that actually matter

If you only read one section, read this one:

1. **Panel type** (OLED, QD-OLED, Mini-LED QLED, regular LED) — determines black levels and contrast
2. **Peak brightness in HDR**, measured in nits — determines whether HDR actually pops in your room
3. **Native refresh rate** (60 Hz, 120 Hz, 144 Hz) — matters for sports, console gaming, PC gaming
4. **HDMI 2.1 ports** (how many, on which inputs) — matters for PS5, Xbox Series X, soundbars
5. **Input lag** in Game Mode — under 15 ms is great, under 10 ms is exceptional
6. **Variable Refresh Rate** (VRR) and ALLM support — only relevant if you game
7. **Built-in audio output** in watts and channel layout — most people will still want a soundbar

Everything else — "AI processor," "1 billion colors," "MotionFlow XR 9000" — is marketing.

## 1. Panel type: the single biggest decision

The panel is the part of the TV that produces the actual picture. Every other spec is downstream of this choice.

**OLED (LG, Sony A-series, Samsung S95)**
Each pixel emits its own light, which means it can switch off completely. Result: perfect blacks, infinite contrast, gorgeous in a dim room. Trade-off: peak brightness still tops out lower than the brightest Mini-LED sets, and a small risk of permanent burn-in if you leave a static image on screen for hundreds of hours.

**QD-OLED (Samsung S95, Sony A95)**
OLED with a quantum-dot color layer. Wider color volume, brighter highlights. Currently the best all-around picture quality you can buy in 2026.

**Mini-LED QLED (Samsung QN90, TCL QM8, Hisense U8)**
Thousands of tiny LED zones behind a quantum-dot LCD panel. Gets *much* brighter than OLED — often 2,000+ nits — which is great for sunny rooms and for HDR content with bright highlights. The trade-off is "blooming," a faint halo around bright objects on dark backgrounds.

**Standard LED LCD (everything cheaper than ~$500 at 55")**
Edge-lit or with a small number of dimming zones. Picture is fine in a bright room, mediocre in a dark one. Buy this only if budget is the deciding factor.

For most living rooms, the practical choice in 2026 is between **QD-OLED** (best picture, best for movies, dim rooms) and **Mini-LED QLED** (brightest, best for sports and bright rooms, generally cheaper). You can see direct head-to-head comparisons of specific models on [VersusNest's TV category](https://versusnest.com), which lists model numbers and side-by-side specs.

## 2. Peak brightness — measured in nits, not "HDR-ready"

"HDR-ready" or "HDR10 compatible" is a meaningless label. What matters is how bright the TV can *actually* get when displaying an HDR highlight.

- **Under 400 nits** — HDR will look almost identical to SDR. Don't bother paying extra for HDR support.
- **400–700 nits** — entry-level HDR. Sun glints and bright skies will have some pop.
- **700–1,200 nits** — most current OLEDs land here. Beautiful HDR in a controlled room.
- **1,500–2,500 nits** — premium Mini-LED and high-end QD-OLED. HDR genuinely looks different.
- **3,000+ nits** — a handful of 2026 flagships. Diminishing returns unless you have a very bright room.

If a manufacturer doesn't publish a peak brightness number, that itself is a signal — it's probably under 400 nits.

## 3. Refresh rate: only matters if you game or watch sports

A 60 Hz TV is fine for movies and most TV. The film industry shoots at 24 fps; doubling, tripling, or quintupling that just produces "soap opera effect" if motion smoothing is on.

You need 120 Hz if:

- You own a PS5 or Xbox Series X and want to play games at 120 fps
- You watch a lot of fast-action sports and care about motion clarity
- You connect a gaming PC

You need 144 Hz only if you're using the TV as a primary PC monitor.

Be careful with the marketing term **"effective refresh rate"** — Samsung's "Motion Rate 240" and TCL's "Motion Rate 480" do not mean 240 Hz or 480 Hz native. The number you want is the **native panel refresh rate**, which is almost always 60 Hz or 120 Hz on consumer TVs in 2026.

## 4. HDMI 2.1 — count the ports, not just the version

HDMI 2.1 is what carries 4K at 120 Hz, VRR, ALLM, and 48 Gbps bandwidth signals. Many TVs advertise "HDMI 2.1" but only one of the four ports is actually full-bandwidth 2.1 — the rest are 2.0. If you have a console, a PC, *and* a soundbar with eARC, you need at least two true HDMI 2.1 ports.

Always look up the **port table** for the specific model, not the marketing page. VersusNest publishes per-model HDMI port breakdowns on each [individual product page](https://versusnest.com).

## 5. Input lag in Game Mode

Input lag is the time between pressing a button and seeing the result on screen. For competitive gaming, anything under 15 ms is fine, under 10 ms is excellent. OLED panels almost universally beat LCD here because of the near-instant pixel response time.

If you don't game, ignore this spec.

## 6. VRR and ALLM

- **VRR (Variable Refresh Rate)** — TV refresh rate matches your console/PC frame rate, eliminating screen tearing.
- **ALLM (Auto Low Latency Mode)** — TV automatically switches to Game Mode when it detects a console.

Both are HDMI 2.1 features. If you game, both are non-negotiable in 2026.

## 7. Audio: most TVs are bad at sound

Built-in TV audio in 2026 is still mostly bad. Even premium models with downward-firing 60W speakers can't compete with a $250 soundbar. Don't pay extra for "Dolby Atmos speakers" inside the TV chassis — put that money into a real soundbar.

If you absolutely won't add a soundbar, look for at least 40W total output and a 2.1 or 2.2 channel layout (meaning a built-in subwoofer driver).

## A quick comparison framework you can use

When you're cross-shopping two specific TVs, build a tiny scorecard like this:

| Spec | TV A | TV B |
|------|------|------|
| Panel type | QD-OLED | Mini-LED |
| Peak HDR brightness (nits) | 1,500 | 2,400 |
| Native refresh rate | 120 Hz | 144 Hz |
| HDMI 2.1 ports | 4 | 2 |
| Input lag (Game Mode) | 9 ms | 13 ms |
| VRR / ALLM | Yes / Yes | Yes / Yes |
| Audio (W, channels) | 60W 4.2.2 | 40W 2.1 |
| Price | $2,199 | $1,499 |

Now you have a real apples-to-apples comparison. Most "TV reviews" don't bother — they pick a winner and move on. A spec table forces you to confront the actual trade-offs.

## Specs you can safely ignore

- **"Contrast ratio: 100,000,000:1"** — meaningless without the measurement methodology
- **"AI Picture Processor"** — every TV has one; the marketing name is irrelevant
- **"Billion+ colors"** — bit depth is what matters here, look for "10-bit panel"
- **"MotionFlow / TruMotion"** — these are motion smoothing settings, not panel specs
- **"Cinema mode certified by [director]"** — pure marketing
- **"Smart TV OS"** — every modern TV runs a smart OS, just plug in a $50 streaming stick if you don't like it

## Frequently asked questions

**Is OLED still worth it in 2026?**
Yes — for dim rooms and movie-heavy viewing, QD-OLED is still the picture-quality leader. The burn-in risk is dramatically lower than it was five years ago and the warranty terms have improved.

**Should I wait for the 2027 models?**
Generally no. The annual TV refresh cycle produces only marginal improvements year-to-year. The bigger savings come from buying the previous year's flagship a few months after the new one launches.

**Does TV size matter for picture quality?**
Same model, same series — picture quality is essentially identical across sizes. The main difference is price-per-inch.

**Can I trust YouTube TV reviews?**
The honest reviewers measure peak brightness with a colorimeter, publish input lag numbers, and don't take sponsorship money from manufacturers. The dishonest ones describe everything as "stunning." Look for the ones who publish charts.
